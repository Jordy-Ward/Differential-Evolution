\begin{abstract}
    Differential evolution (DE) algorithms have been developed to solve continuous-valued optimisation problems. Interestingly, the canonical DE algorithm utilises discrete 
    recombination crossover to build child vectors throughout the life span of the algorithm. Advanced continuous crossover operators are implemented in this report 
    and tested against eight multi-modal benchmark functions to determine if crossover mechanisms intentionally designed for  
    real-valued representations outperform standard discrete recombination. By specifically implementing blending crossover operators and using  
    the operators in DE algorithms, a significant improvement in continuous optimisation performance and accuracy is hypothesised. Experimental evaluation was performed 
    at 30 dimensions over 30 independent runs for 3000 generations each. Results indicate that continuous blending methods, particularly those utilising expanded barycentric and normal distributions, significantly outperform the discrete baseline on macro-gradient and deceptive landscapes. However, exploitative  parent-centric methods remain vulnerable to premature convergence.  
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
    
    Differential evolution (DE) \cite{storn1997} is a population-based stochastic optimisation algorithm widely 
    utilised for solving complex, non-linear continuous optimisation problems. The algorithm 
    improves a candidate population iteratively through mutation, recombination and selection. While DE 
    has proven highly successful, the canonical formulation of the algorithm contains a fundamental geometric 
    contradiction when applied to continuous landscapes.

    The standard DE algorithm-such as the widely used DE/rand/2/bin variant-relies on discrete 
    binomial recombination to generate child vectors. Binomial crossover operates by independently swapping exact parameter values between a target vector and a mutated trial vector. While computationally efficient, this discrete swapping mechanism restricts the search trajectory 
    of the algorithm to a rigid grid, which limits the ability of the algorithm to effectively navigate continuous, non 
    separable landscapes.

    To overcome the geometric limitations of discrete recombination, real-valued blending 
    crossover operators have been developed. Operators such as uniform arithmetic crossover (AX), 
    simplex crossover (SPX), unimodal normal distribution crossover (UNDX), and parent-centric crossover (PCX), generate offspring within a continuous mathematical space, such as barycentric 
    expanded triangles or multi-dimensional Gaussian ellipsoids. Because these methods generate new vector geometry defined by the relative positions of the parent vectors rather than the 
    axes of the search grid, continuous blending crossover operators are coordinate-independent and offer highly 
    specialised mechanisms for both 
    local exploitation and global exploration. 

    The primary goal of this report is to investigate the efficacy of integrating continuous blending crossover operators into the standard DE algorithm. 
    By replacing the binomial crossover with AX, SPX, UNDX, and PCX this report evaluates whether real-valued recombination outperforms discrete parameter 
    swapping. The modified algorithms were benchmarked across eight multi-modal, 30-dimensional functions. Experimental observations confirmed that continuous 
    blending crossover operators significantly enhanced performance on specific topographies. AX and UNDX demonstrated superior exploitation on macro-gradient 
    landscapes, while the expanded exploratory geometry of SPX bypassed deceptive geometric traps. Conversely, highly exploitative operators such as PCX were 
    uniquely vulnerable to premature convergence on multi-modal surfaces.

    The remainder of this report is organised as follows. Section two details the mathematical formulations of the implemented crossover operators. S
    ection three outlines the experimental setup, benchmark functions, and testing parameters. Section four presents convergence data and provides a 
    comparative analysis of algorithm performance. Section five concludes the report and summarises key findings. 


\section{Background}

    Continuous optimisation involves finding the global minimum or maximum of an objective function within a multi-dimensional search 
    space. Multi-modal geometric landscapes contain many local optima that trap traditional gradient-based 
    search methods. NOTE: ADD WHAT I MEAN BY TRADITIONAL GRADIENT BASED SEARCH METHODS. Stochastic metaheuristic algorithms address this challenge on complex landscapes. 
    Metaheuristic algorithms must balance broad search across the landscape with local refinement of a candidate solution, to converge on the global solution.  

    The broader field of evolutionary algorithms (EAs) is built upon several foundational paradigms. Genetic algorithms were conceptualized and 
    popularized by the works of Fraser \cite{fraser1957}, Bremermann \cite{bremermann1962}, 
    Reed et al. \cite{reed1967}, and Holland \cite{holland1975}. Concurrently, 
    evolutionary programming was introduced by L.J. Fogel \cite{fogel_lj1962} and later extended to 
    continuous-valued representations by D.B. Fogel \cite{fogel_db1995}. Furthermore, Rechenberg 
    \cite{rechenberg1973} and Schwefel \cite{schwefel1977} pioneered evolution strategies, a paradigm 
    inherently reliant on real-valued deviations.

    Differential evolution (DE) is a prominent subset of evolutionary algorithms (EAs) explicitly developed to solve continuous-valued 
    optimisation problems.  Like traditional evolutionary algorithms, DE utilises a population of candidate solutions that evolve over successive 
    generations. However, unlike other evolutionary algorithms that apply selection before mutation, DE performs the mutation of a random vector 
    before applying selection. The mutation process itself also differs in the manner that mutation step sizes are not sampled 
    from a prior known probability distribution function but rather, the mutation step size is influenced by the differences between 
    individuals of the current population. 

    The canonical DE algorithm, frequently denoted by the mutation and crossover strategy DE/rand/1/bin, operates through a continuous 
    loop of four primary phases: initialisation, mutation, recombination, and selection. During mutation, a trial vector is created by 
    adding together a weighted difference of randomly selected population vectors to the target vector. Recombination then mixes the 
    parameters of the trial vector and the target vector to create offspring vectors. Finally an elitist selection mechanism compares 
    the target vector to the spawned offspring vector, keeping strictly the candidate that yields the better fitness. The high level 
    execution of a standard DE algorithm is illustrated in Algorithm \ref{alg:canonical_de}. 

    \begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Standard Differential Evolution}
\label{alg:canonical_de}
\begin{algorithmic}[1] 
\STATE \textbf{Initialize} population $P = \{\mathbf{x}_1, \dots, \mathbf{x}_{NP}\}$ randomly within search bounds
\WHILE{termination condition is not met}
    \FOR{$i \in \{1, \dots, NP\}$}
        \STATE \textit{// Mutation}
        \STATE Select distinct random indices from the population, excluding $i$
        \STATE Generate a trial vector $\mathbf{v}_i$ using vector differences and scale factor $F$
        
        \STATE \textit{// Recombination}
        \STATE Generate an offspring vector $\mathbf{u}_i$ by recombining the trial vector $\mathbf{v}_i$ and target $\mathbf{x}_i$ based on crossover rate $CR$
        
        \STATE \textit{// Selection}
        \STATE Evaluate the fitness of the offspring $f(\mathbf{u}_i)$
        \IF{$f(\mathbf{u}_i) \leq f(\mathbf{x}_i)$}
            \STATE $\mathbf{x}_i^{next} \gets \mathbf{u}_i$
        \ELSE
            \STATE $\mathbf{x}_i^{next} \gets \mathbf{x}_i$
        \ENDIF
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE $P \gets P^{next}$
\ENDWHILE
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

    The recombination phase is critical for injecting diversity and guiding the search geometry. In standard DE, diversity injection is achieved using 
    discrete operators like binomial or exponential crossover. Metaheuristic algorithms build offspring vectors by independently swapping 
    exact parameter values along the search axes. The discrete parameter-swapping approach is coordinate dependent and struggles on rotated or non-separable 
    landscapes. To address this limitation, extensive literature within the domain of real-coded genetic algorithms (RCGAs) has explored 
    continuous blending operators. Instead of swapping axis coordinates, these continuous operators create offspring by exploring 
    the mathematical volume or geometry between parents. By utilizing techniques like arithmetic interpolation, simplex expansions, 
    or Gaussian probability distributions, the geometric space between target vectors is explored in finer detail.


\section{Methodology}

To investigate the performance differences between discrete and continuous blending recombination, a baseline DE/rand/2 
DE framework was implemented in Python. The standard binomial crossover was established as the 
baseline solver. Subsequently, four distinct continuous blending crossover operators were implemented by exclusively 
modifying the recombination phase of the algorithm. The mutation and selection methods were left constant.

\subsection{Baseline: Binomial Crossover}
The control algorithm utilized the standard discrete binomial crossover. For a given target vector $\mathbf{x}_i(t)$ and 
a mutant donor vector $\mathbf{v}_i(t)$, the trial vector $\mathbf{u}_i(t)$ was constructed by independently evaluating 
a uniform random number against the crossover rate, $CR$, for each dimension $j$. To prevent stagnation, a randomly 
selected index $j_{rand}$ was guaranteed to inherit from the donor vector.
The exact pseudocode for this procedure is outlined in Algorithm \ref{alg:binomial_crossover}.
    
\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Binomial Crossover}
\label{alg:binomial_crossover}
\begin{algorithmic}[1] 
\REQUIRE Target vector $\mathbf{x}_i$, Trial vector $\mathbf{v}_i$, Crossover Rate $CR$, Dimension $D$
\ENSURE Off spring vector $\mathbf{u}_i$
\STATE $j_{rand} \gets \text{random integer in } [1, D]$
\FOR{$j \in \{1, \dots, D\}$}
    \STATE $r \gets \text{random uniform number in } [0, 1]$
    \IF{$r < CR$ \OR $j = j_{rand}$}
        \STATE $u_{i,j} \gets v_{i,j}$  \textit{ // Inherit from trial vector}
    \ELSE
        \STATE $u_{i,j} \gets x_{i,j}$  \textit{ // Inherit from target}
    \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\RETURN $\mathbf{u}_i$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Uniform Arithmetic Crossover}
To implement a highly explorative continuous blend, a uniform arithmetic operator was formulated \cite{michalewicz1992}. Unlike standard 
linear arithmetic crossover which applies a single scalar across all dimensions, this implementation decoupled the 
blending weights. For every dimension $j$, an independent random scalar $\gamma_j \in [0, 1]$ was sampled:
\begin{equation}
u_{ij}(t) = (1 - \gamma_j)x_{ij}(t) + \gamma_j v_{ij}(t)
\end{equation}
Geometrically, this independent scaling guarantees the offspring are sampled uniformly from the multi-dimensional hyper-rectangle bounded 
by the target and donor vectors.
The execution of this blend is detailed in Algorithm \ref{alg:ax_crossover}.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Uniform Arithmetic Crossover}
\label{alg:ax_crossover}
\begin{algorithmic}[1] 
\REQUIRE Target vector $\mathbf{x}_i$, Trial vector vector $\mathbf{v}_i$, Dimension $D$
\ENSURE Offspring vector $\mathbf{u}_i$
\FOR{$j \in \{1, \dots, D\}$}
    \STATE $\gamma_j \gets \text{random uniform number in } [0, 1]$
    \STATE $u_{i,j} \gets (1 - \gamma_j) \cdot x_{i,j} + \gamma_j \cdot v_{i,j}$
\ENDFOR
\RETURN $\mathbf{u}_i$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Simplex Crossover}
The simplex operator \cite{tsutsui1999} was implemented using $m=3$ parents: the target vector, the donor vector, and a randomly selected 
third vector from the population. The center of mass $\mathbf{O}$ of these three vertices was calculated, and the 
simplex was expanded outward using an expansion rate of $\epsilon = \sqrt{3}$. Offspring were uniformly sampled from 
within this expanded barycentric geometry by generating two random weights, $r_1$ and $r_2$, shown in Figure \ref{fig:spx_viz}. A fold trick was 
applied, where if $r_1 + r_2 > 1.0$, the values are inverted as $r_1 = 1 - r_1$ and $r_2 = 1 - r_2$ to ensure uniform interior distribution 
without skewing.
The simplex expansion and sampling process is outlined in Algorithm \ref{alg:spx_crossover}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{../plots/SPX.png}
    \caption{Geometric visualization of the simplex crossover operator.}
    \label{fig:spx_viz}
\end{figure}

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Simplex Crossover}
\label{alg:spx_crossover}
\begin{algorithmic}[1] 
\REQUIRE Target vector $\mathbf{x}_1$, Donor vector $\mathbf{x}_2$, Random vector $\mathbf{x}_3$, Dimension $D$
\ENSURE Trial vector $\mathbf{u}_i$
\STATE $m \gets 3$ \textit{ // Number of parents}
\STATE $\epsilon \gets \sqrt{m}$ \textit{ // Expansion rate for three parents}

\STATE \textit{// Calculate the center of mass}
\FOR{$j \in \{1, \dots, D\}$}
    \STATE $O_j \gets (x_{1,j} + x_{2,j} + x_{3,j}) / 3.0$
\ENDFOR

\STATE \textit{// Calculate the expanded vertices}
\FOR{$j \in \{1, \dots, D\}$}
    \STATE $y_{1,j} \gets O_j + \epsilon \cdot (x_{1,j} - O_j)$
    \STATE $y_{2,j} \gets O_j + \epsilon \cdot (x_{2,j} - O_j)$
    \STATE $y_{3,j} \gets O_j + \epsilon \cdot (x_{3,j} - O_j)$
\ENDFOR

\STATE \textit{// Generate two random weights and uniformly sample inside the triangle}
\STATE $r_1 \gets \text{random uniform number in } [0, 1]$
\STATE $r_2 \gets \text{random uniform number in } [0, 1]$
\IF{$r_1 + r_2 > 1.0$}
    \STATE $r_1 \gets 1.0 - r_1$
    \STATE $r_2 \gets 1.0 - r_2$
\ENDIF

\STATE $w_1 \gets 1.0 - r_1 - r_2$
\STATE $w_2 \gets r_1$
\STATE $w_3 \gets r_2$

\STATE \textit{// Assemble child vector using calculated weights}
\FOR{$j \in \{1, \dots, D\}$}
    \STATE $u_{i,j} \gets w_1 \cdot y_{1,j} + w_2 \cdot y_{2,j} + w_3 \cdot y_{3,j}$
\ENDFOR

\RETURN $\mathbf{u}_i$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Unimodal Normal Distribution Crossover}
The classic three-parent UNDX operator \cite{ono1997} was implemented to search the probability space between solutions, as visualized in Figure \ref{fig:undx_viz}. The normal 
distribution was centered exactly at the midpoint $\mathbf{m}$ between the target and donor vectors, with the primary 
axis $\mathbf{d}$ defined as the vector connecting the two parents. 

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{../plots/UNDX.png}
    \caption{Geometric visualization of the unimodal normal distribution crossover.}
    \label{fig:undx_viz}
\end{figure}

Strictly implementing UNDX requires constructing a full orthonormal basis matrix for the remaining dimensions, 
an $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ operation computationally prohibitive for a high-frequency DE loop. To optimize performance, 
a randomized orthogonal projection shortcut was implemented. A single vector $\mathbf{z}$ consisting of standard 
Gaussian noise was generated, and its projection onto the primary axis $\mathbf{d}$ was subtracted to yield the 
purely orthogonal noise component $\mathbf{z}_{orth}$. The offspring vector was constructed as:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{u}_i(t) = \mathbf{m} + \xi\mathbf{d} + \sigma_\eta d_{orth} \mathbf{z}_{orth}
\end{equation}
where primary variance $\xi \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.5^2)$, $d_{orth}$ is the orthogonal distance of the third parent to the 
primary axis, and orthogonal variance is scaled by $\sigma_\eta = 0.35 / \sqrt{n_{dim}}$.
The complete sequence for the UNDX spatial projection is provided in Algorithm \ref{alg:undx_crossover_mid}.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Unimodal Normal Distribution Crossover}
\label{alg:undx_crossover_mid}
\begin{algorithmic}[1] 
\REQUIRE Target vector $\mathbf{x}_1$, Donor vector $\mathbf{x}_2$, Random vector $\mathbf{x}_3$, Dimension $D$
\ENSURE Trial vector $\mathbf{u}_i$
\STATE $\sigma_\xi \gets 0.5$
\STATE $\sigma_\eta \gets 0.35 / \sqrt{D}$

\STATE \textit{// 1. Define the primary search axis}
\STATE $\mathbf{m} \gets \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}_1 + \mathbf{x}_2)$
\STATE $\mathbf{d} \gets \mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_2$

\STATE \textit{// 2. Calculate orthogonal distance $d_{orth}$ from $\mathbf{x}_3$}
\STATE $\mathbf{v} \gets \mathbf{x}_3 - \mathbf{x}_1$
\STATE $\mathbf{v}_{proj} \gets \left( \frac{\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{d}}{\mathbf{d} \cdot \mathbf{d}} \right) \mathbf{d}$ \textit{ // Project $\mathbf{v}$ onto the primary axis}
\STATE $\mathbf{v}_{orth} \gets \mathbf{v} - \mathbf{v}_{proj}$
\STATE $d_{orth} \gets \|\mathbf{v}_{orth}\|$ \textit{ // Magnitude of the orthogonal component}

\STATE \textit{// 3. Generate orthogonal Gaussian noise vector}
\STATE $\mathbf{z} \gets$ random vector of length $D$ sampled from $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$
\STATE $\mathbf{z}_{proj} \gets \left( \frac{\mathbf{z} \cdot \mathbf{d}}{\mathbf{d} \cdot \mathbf{d}} \right) \mathbf{d}$
\STATE $\mathbf{z}_{orth} \gets \mathbf{z} - \mathbf{z}_{proj}$

\STATE \textit{// 4. Assemble the trial vector}
\STATE $\xi \gets \text{random number sampled from } \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\xi^2)$
\STATE $\mathbf{u}_i \gets \mathbf{m} + \xi\mathbf{d} + \sigma_\eta \cdot d_{orth} \cdot \mathbf{z}_{orth}$

\RETURN $\mathbf{u}_i$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Parent-Centric Crossover}
To evaluate an exploitative continuous operator, the Generalized PCX algorithm \cite{deb2002} was integrated, as visualized in Figure \ref{fig:pcx_viz}. The collective center 
of mass $\mathbf{O}$ for the three parents was calculated, and the primary direction vector $\mathbf{d}_i$ was drawn 
from $\mathbf{O}$ strictly toward the primary parent, which is the target vector. 

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{../plots/PCX.png}
    \caption{Geometric visualization of the parent-centric crossover.}
    \label{fig:pcx_viz}
\end{figure}

The average perpendicular distance $\bar{d}$ of the other two parents to this primary axis was computed. Using the 
identical orthogonal projection shortcut ($\mathbf{z}_{orth}$) developed for UNDX, a Gaussian scatter cloud was 
generated explicitly centered on the primary parent. The standard deviations for both the primary and orthogonal 
spreads were fixed at highly exploitative values of $\sigma_\xi = 0.1$ and $\sigma_\eta = 0.1$.
The steps for clustering the offspring around the primary parent are detailed in Algorithm \ref{alg:pcx_crossover}.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Parent-Centric Crossover}
\label{alg:pcx_crossover}
\begin{algorithmic}[1] 
\REQUIRE Target vector $\mathbf{x}_1$ (Primary), Donor vector $\mathbf{x}_2$, Random vector $\mathbf{x}_3$, Dimension $D$
\ENSURE Trial vector $\mathbf{u}_i$
\STATE $\sigma_\xi \gets 0.1$ \textit{ // Highly exploitative primary spread}
\STATE $\sigma_\eta \gets 0.1$ \textit{ // Highly exploitative orthogonal spread}

\STATE \textit{// 1. Calculate Center of Mass and Primary Axis}
\STATE $\mathbf{O} \gets \frac{1}{3}(\mathbf{x}_1 + \mathbf{x}_2 + \mathbf{x}_3)$
\STATE $\mathbf{d} \gets \mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{O}$ \textit{ // Direction vector points strictly toward Primary Parent}

\STATE \textit{// 2. Calculate average orthogonal distance $\bar{d}$ of other parents}
\STATE $\mathbf{v}_2 \gets \mathbf{x}_2 - \mathbf{x}_1$
\STATE $\mathbf{v}_{2, proj} \gets \left( \frac{\mathbf{v}_2 \cdot \mathbf{d}}{\mathbf{d} \cdot \mathbf{d}} \right) \mathbf{d}$
\STATE $d_2 \gets \|\mathbf{v}_2 - \mathbf{v}_{2, proj}\|$

\STATE $\mathbf{v}_3 \gets \mathbf{x}_3 - \mathbf{x}_1$
\STATE $\mathbf{v}_{3, proj} \gets \left( \frac{\mathbf{v}_3 \cdot \mathbf{d}}{\mathbf{d} \cdot \mathbf{d}} \right) \mathbf{d}$
\STATE $d_3 \gets \|\mathbf{v}_3 - \mathbf{v}_{3, proj}\|$

\STATE $\bar{d} \gets \frac{d_2 + d_3}{2}$ \textit{ // Average perpendicular distance}

\STATE \textit{// 3. Generate orthogonal Gaussian noise vector}
\STATE $\mathbf{z} \gets$ random vector of length $D$ sampled from $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$
\STATE $\mathbf{z}_{proj} \gets \left( \frac{\mathbf{z} \cdot \mathbf{d}}{\mathbf{d} \cdot \mathbf{d}} \right) \mathbf{d}$
\STATE $\mathbf{z}_{orth} \gets \mathbf{z} - \mathbf{z}_{proj}$

\STATE \textit{// 4. Assemble the trial vector (Centered exactly on the Primary Parent)}
\STATE $\xi \gets \text{random number sampled from } \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\xi^2)$
\STATE $\mathbf{u}_i \gets \mathbf{x}_1 + \xi\mathbf{d} + \sigma_\eta \cdot \bar{d} \cdot \mathbf{z}_{orth}$

\RETURN $\mathbf{u}_i$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

accuracy, spatial expansion and ability to avoid premature stagnation across a variety of mathematical landscapes.

\subsection{Performance Measures}
The primary performance measure utilized for this study was Average Best Fitness (Accuracy). 
For each iteration, the fitness of the best candidate solution in the population was recorded. Because Differential Evolution 
is a stochastic algorithm, tracking the fitness of a single run is insufficient to draw reliable conclusions. Therefore, the 
best fitness values were averaged across all independent runs at each iteration step. This averaging methodology allowed for the generation of average 
convergence curves to visually and quantitatively assess the final accuracy of each crossover variant.

\subsection{Experimental Setup and Statistical Analysis}
To account for the stochastic nature of evolutionary algorithms, each of the five DE variants was executed for 
30 independent runs on all eight benchmark functions. The initialization bounds for the first generation were strictly 
enforced according to the standard domain constraints of each respective benchmark function, as detailed in Table 
\ref{tab:benchmarks}. To formally validate the observed performance disparities, a 
non-parametric Mann-Whitney U test ($\alpha = 0.05$) was conducted to compare the 
final fitness distributions of the leading crossover operators against the discrete 
baseline. The complete hypothesis-test summary is provided in Section 
\ref{sec:stat_sig_tests}, Table \ref{tab:p_values}.

\section{Research Results}
The empirical findings of the continuous crossover operator study are presented in this section. Average 
convergence behaviours of the five DE variants were analysed across 3000 iterations to evaluate relative exploitation and exploration capabilities. 
Based on benchmark topological characteristics, two 
distinct performance narratives emerged: dominance on macro-gradient landscapes and resilience on deceptive landscapes.
For completeness and reproducibility, full descriptive statistics for all solver-function combinations are reported in Appendix C, Table \ref{tab:summary_stats}.

\subsection{Performance on Macro-Gradient Landscapes}
On benchmark functions characterized by a strong underlying macro-gradient directing the search toward a 
global basin—specifically Ackley, Alpine 1, Griewank, Levy, and Salomon—a clear performance hierarchy was 
observed. 

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{../plots/Alpine1_convergence.png}
    \caption{Average convergence profiles on the Alpine 1 function.}
    \label{fig:alpine_convergence}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:alpine_convergence} and Figure \ref{fig:ackley_convergence} show that uniform arithmetic crossover,
denoted as DE/rand/2/AX, and unimodal normal distribution crossover, denoted as DE/rand/2/UNDX, vastly outperformed 
the remaining variants. Both AX and UNDX exhibited near-instantaneous convergence to the global optimum 
within the first 200 iterations and demonstrated statistically significant superiority over the baseline ($p < 0.05$). The mathematical formulation of AX and UNDX supports interpolative sampling of 
continuous space between parents, making both crossover mechanisms highly efficient exploiters when a reliable landscape gradient points toward the minimum.


\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{../plots/Ackley_convergence.png}
    \caption{Average convergence profiles on the Ackley function.}
    \label{fig:ackley_convergence}
\end{figure}

Conversely, simplex crossover, denoted as DE/rand/2/SPX, demonstrated severe premature stagnation on 
the same landscapes. Across Ackley, Alpine 1, Griewank, Levy, and Salomon, SPX rapidly 
descended during initial iterations but flatlined before reaching the global optimum. 
SPX stagnated near an accuracy of 7.0 on Ackley and Alpine 1. Figure \ref{fig:ackley_box} shows that final fitness distributions on Ackley reflected a 
clear reliability gap. AX and UNDX converged to the global optimum with near-zero variance, while the interquartile range of SPX remained elevated. 
Ackley contains a tight and deep global minimum basin, and successful exploitation requires strict contraction of local search volume. 
Figure \ref{fig:ackley_box} showed that SPX failure was a consistent geometric penalty rather than an outlier effect. The continuous expansion 
of search geometry from the center of mass by a factor of $\sqrt{3}$ removed the local contractive variance required to settle into a macro-gradient basin.

\subsection{Resilience on Deceptive and Highly Multimodal Landscapes}
The structural bias of the algorithms shifted when evaluated on highly deceptive, 
multimodal topologies, specifically Michalewicz, Rastrigin, and Schwefel. 
On these landscapes, high-frequency local minima act as severe traps for heavily exploitative operators.

Convergence plots showed that DE/rand/2/SPX was the strongest performer on these complex surfaces. 
On Michalewicz, SPX successfully bypassed the steep directional ridges to achieve a final median fitness of roughly 
-20.5, diving significantly deeper than the -11.8 of the discrete baseline ($p < 0.05$). The value of expanded barycentric geometry 
was apparent when examining the performance distribution on highly deceptive topologies. Michalewicz features steep, 
directional valleys and ridges that heavily penalize algorithms lacking coordinate independence. While the convergence 
curves demonstrated superior average depth for SPX, and the Michalewicz boxplot in Figure \ref{fig:michalewicz_box} revealed 
structural vulnerability of the other operators. Highly exploitative algorithms like PCX and AX exhibit incredibly tight 
interquartile ranges at much poorer fitness levels, indicating consistent, premature convergence into the exact same local 
traps across all 30 independent runs. In contrast, the slightly wider variance of SPX at a much deeper fitness level 
demonstrated the exploratory resilience of the crossover operator. Rather than collapsing into a single false ridge, expanded geometry allowed 
SPX to consistently navigate into the deepest region of the search space.

\subsection{Limitations of Parent-Centric and Discrete Operators}

Across the entire benchmark suite, the baseline discrete binomial crossover and the 
continuous parent-centric crossover consistently struggled to match the leading algorithms.

The discrete parameter-swapping nature of the binomial baseline restricted the search trajectory to a 
rigid coordinate grid causing step-like convergence on smooth, non-separable functions 
like Griewank, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:griewank_convergence}, and Levy. While PCX operates in continuous space, the highly localized anchor on the 
primary parent proved far too exploitative. Because spatial variance was strictly limited, PCX lacked 
the geometric flexibility to explore broadly, frequently resulting in premature stagnation on both flat and complex landscapes. However, it 
must be acknowledged that PCX was deliberately configured with highly exploitative static 
hyperparameters ($\sigma_\xi = 0.1$ and $\sigma_\eta = 0.1$) for this study; dynamic parameter 
adaptation or a broader initial variance could potentially alter its geometric resilience on 
these multimodal landscapes.

\section{Conclusion}
This study investigated whether continuous blending crossover operators, including uniform arithmetic crossover (AX), unimodal normal distribution crossover (UNDX), parent-centric crossover (PCX), and simplex crossover (SPX), could overcome the geometric 
limitations of canonical discrete binomial recombination within the differential evolution (DE) framework. The operators were 
evaluated across a rigorous suite of eight 30-dimensional benchmark functions to assess their relative exploration and exploitation 
capabilities over 3000 iterations. 

The empirical results confirmed the core hypothesis: continuous recombination provides a significant advantage over discrete 
parameter swapping, though algorithmic superiority is strictly dictated by landscape topology. Operators that interpolatively sample 
the space between parents, such as AX and UNDX, demonstrated vastly superior exploitation on macro-gradient topologies, achieving 
near-instantaneous convergence. However, these same operators proved vulnerable to premature stagnation on deceptive landscapes. 
In contrast, the expanded barycentric sampling of SPX provided unmatched geometric resilience, allowing the algorithm to bypass 
severe local traps on highly multimodal surfaces like Rastrigin and Michalewicz. Highly localized operators, specifically PCX 
and the discrete baseline, consistently underperformed due to insufficient spatial variance. 

A primary learning outcome from this implementation is that algorithmic success relies heavily on matching the continuous 
search geometry to the specific topological challenges of the environment. Moving from a discrete grid to a continuous volume 
is not a universal solution if the spatial variance is not carefully calibrated to prevent population collapse. This conclusion 
is supported by the full per-function distribution summaries in Appendix C, Table \ref{tab:summary_stats}, and by the hypothesis-test outcomes 
in Appendix D, Table \ref{tab:p_values}. 

Future work should explore dynamic, adaptive strategies that shift between exploitative operators, such as UNDX, 
and exploratory operators, such as SPX, based on real-time landscape feedback. Future work should also 
hybridize geometric DE crossover mechanisms with particle swarm optimisation (PSO) architectures, combining 
the spatial diversity of barycentric expansion with swarm-velocity momentum to address high-dimensional continuous domains.
